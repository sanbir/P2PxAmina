P2PxAmina — Proposed Smart Contract Architecture

Design exploration · v0.1 · companion to Segregated Institutional Lending Protocol v0.7

1. Design constraints unique to this protocol

The product brief in P2PxAmina-lending-protocol-for-banks describes a system that looks like a lending market but obeys a different set of rules than AAVE or Compound. The contract design must reflect those rules — borrowing AAVE/Compound primitives blindly will produce something that is over-engineered for the actual scope and yet missing the right primitives.

The constraints, in declining order of architectural impact:

#ConstraintArchitectural consequence
1Bilateral, not pooled. Each loan is a deal between a specific lender and a specific borrower. No commingling of risk.No utilization curve. No share token. No pro-rata loss socialisation. Each deal is its own escrow.
2Fixed term, fixed rate. Rate is set off-chain by AMINA and frozen into the deal at creation.No interest-rate strategy contract. Interest is linear, computed on-the-fly from (principal, rate, startTs, nowTs).
3Permissioned counterparties. Every address acting as lender, borrower, or collateral source has been KYB'd by AMINA under a FINMA licence.Identity gate at every state-changing entry point. Supply & collateral tokens are themselves permissioned (ERC-3643 or allow-list-flavoured ERC-20).
4Permissioned liquidator. Only AMINA can liquidate. Recovery is "AMINA redeems collateral at custody," not a Dutch auction.No permissionless liquidationCall. No bonus curve. No keeper economics. Liquidation is a privileged, deterministic settlement.
5Tokens are claims on custody. Supply tokens (Token B) and collateral tokens (Token A) are minted 1:1 by regulated custodians against assets they hold.The on-chain layer never holds the "real" asset. The trust anchor is the custodian's redemption, not on-chain reserves. Token whitelist becomes a critical governance surface.
6Matching is off-chain. Lenders and borrowers do not see each other. AMINA's licensed brokerage matches them; the chain only records the result.The contract never picks counterparties. Deals arrive on-chain already matched, with EIP-712 signatures from both sides + AMINA's broker attestation.
7Privacy by separation. Pool-style accounting would leak counterparty information through share-token graphs.Per-deal escrow gives natural pseudonymity. Counterparties don't share any on-chain state that links them, beyond the deal record itself (which is the minimum legally required record).
8~1,000–1,500 lines of Solidity. The v0.7 brief explicitly bounds the scope.Discipline. Resist the urge to port AAVE v4's full hub/spoke split, position-manager layer, and dynamic config matrix. Use the patterns; trim the surface.
One-line summary. Build a fixed-term bilateral repo escrow with a permissioned identity gate, a single privileged liquidator, and an off-chain matching brain — borrowing AAVE v4's structural ideas (configurator/role separation, oracle isolation, dynamic risk versioning) without inheriting its pool-based economic machinery.

3. Alternative architectures we considered

Three credible designs were on the table before settling on the recommendation in §4. Documenting them keeps the trade-offs explicit.

Design A — Per-deal proxy clone

Each deal is its own minimal proxy (EIP-1167) pointing at a shared DealEscrow implementation. The factory mints proxies on settlement.

  • Pro — maximum isolation. A bug or upgrade affecting one deal cannot affect another. Storage of each deal is physically separate. Privacy: each deal is its own address on the chain explorer.
  • Pro — clean lifecycle: deal closes → proxy self-destructs (or marks itself terminal), permanent record on-chain.
  • Con — gas: ~50k extra per deal for the proxy deploy. For institutional volume (deals worth $100k+) this is irrelevant in cost but produces address-explorer noise.
  • Con — operational: monitoring across N proxies needs an off-chain index. AMINA's curator UI needs a list endpoint.
  • Verdict — strongest pure-design choice, but heavier than the v0.7 scope warrants. Worth reconsidering in v2 if deals become per-counterparty bespoke.

Design B — Compound-style per-collateral-class market

One LendingEngine per (supply token × collateral class). All deals borrowing USDC against BTC collateral live in one contract; USDC-against-ETH lives in another.

  • Pro — natural risk isolation by collateral class. Compromising the BTC oracle does not touch ETH deals.
  • Pro — well-trodden pattern (Comet did exactly this).
  • Con — deal-level data still has to be per-deal inside each market. The market boundary buys little once you accept the constraint of bilateral fixed-term deals.
  • Con — N markets is N audit surfaces, and each new collateral type requires a new deployment.
  • Verdict — overkill for v1 (which targets BTC/ETH/USDC). Right call if the product expands into many heterogeneous RWA collateral classes.

Design C — single shared engine, deal as struct

One LendingEngine contract. Each deal is a dealId → Deal entry in a mapping. All state transitions go through the same entry points, gated by deal-state checks.

  • Pro — smallest contract surface and audit footprint. Fits the 1,000–1,500 LOC budget naturally.
  • Pro — easiest to reason about — one engine, one set of invariants.
  • Pro — composability: cross-deal operations (batch repay, portfolio view) are trivially cheap.
  • Con — isolation is logical, not physical. A reentrancy or storage-collision bug touches every deal at once.
  • Con — privacy is by indirection only. All deals share one address, so explorer-level inspection is easier than with Design A.
  • Verdict — recommended for v1. Mitigate the isolation downside with strict per-deal state guards, a non-reentrant skeleton, and a paused/halted kill switch.
Recommendation: Design C with the option to migrate to Design A in v2 by factoring the Deal struct into a per-deal contract via the existing EscrowVault abstraction.

5. Contract-by-contract specification

L1 · governance

RoleManager

Thin wrapper around OpenZeppelin's AccessManager. Defines the canonical roles. Every restricted function elsewhere in the system delegates its check here.

RoleHolderCan do
GOVERNORP2P Staking multisiggrantRole, contract upgrades, emergency unpause, whitelist new contracts.
CURATORAMINA Bank multisigAdd/update collateral pairs, set LTVs, set per-issuer parameters, approve KYB.
LIQUIDATORAMINA bot wallets (rotatable)Call warn, partialLiquidate, fullLiquidate on deals.
EMERGENCYJoint: P2P + AMINA (2-of-2)Pause specific deals, pause a collateral pair, halt the entire engine.
ORACLE_ADMINAMINA + Chainlink opsRegister / rotate price feeds. Cannot deactivate a feed mid-deal.

Why explicit roles instead of onlyOwner: AMINA's regulatory posture requires a paper trail of who-changed-what, and AMINA's hot wallets rotate. AccessManager gives both for free.

L1 · identity

KYBGateway

Maps an EVM address to a KYB status. P2P collects KYB documents off-chain; AMINA reviews under its FINMA banking licence and writes the final status on-chain.

enum KybStatus { Unknown, Approved, Suspended, Revoked }

struct KybRecord {
    KybStatus status;
    uint64 approvedAt;          // ts of last approval
    uint64 reviewedUntil;       // expiry — periodic re-attestation
    bytes32 documentsHash;      // hash of off-chain KYB packet
    address approvedBy;         // AMINA signer who attested
}

mapping(address => KybRecord) public records;

function setStatus(address wallet, KybStatus s, bytes32 docsHash, uint64 until)
    external onlyRole(CURATOR);

function require Approved(address wallet) external view;  // reverts if not approved or expired

Reviewed-until expiry is a quiet but important detail: regulators require periodic re-attestation. An AMINA-approved wallet that has not been re-reviewed in N months should fail the gate without requiring a separate Suspended write.

Every state-changing entry point in LendingEngine calls KYBGateway.requireApproved(msg.sender) and, for openDeal, also requireApproved(counterparty). This is the protocol's hard "no anon users" boundary.

L1 · registry

IssuerRegistry

The list of custodians and their tokens. Every supply or collateral token used in a deal must be registered here.

struct TokenInfo {
    address custodian;          // who mints/burns
    TokenKind kind;             // Supply or Collateral
    uint8 decimals;             // cached
    bool paused;                // emergency stop for this token
    bytes32 attestationHash;    // pointer to off-chain redemption guarantee
}

mapping(address => TokenInfo) public tokens;

Two invariants enforced at deal creation:

  1. Supply tokens (Token B) must have kind = Supply; collateral tokens (Token A) must have kind = Collateral. A token issued for one purpose cannot be silently repurposed for the other.
  2. The token is not paused. Pausing a token at the registry blocks new deals using it but leaves existing deals operating (so existing deals can still be repaid or liquidated).

The registry is the single source of truth that connects the on-chain world to the custody assertion "this token is backed 1:1." If AMINA delists a Fireblocks-issued token here, the protocol stops accepting it for new deals instantly.

L2 · risk

OracleRouter

Per-token price feed registry. Modelled on AAVE's AaveOracle but per-collateral-pair rather than per-Spoke.

struct Feed {
    address source;             // Chainlink aggregator or composite adapter
    uint32 heartbeat;           // max staleness in seconds
    uint8 sourceDecimals;       // 8 for Chainlink USD pairs
    bool active;
}

mapping(address => Feed) public feeds;

function getUsdValue(address token, uint256 amount) external view returns (uint256);

For tokenised BTC LSTs (LBTC, eBTC) the feed should be a composite adapter (LBTC/BTC rate × BTC/USD Chainlink), with a CAPO-style cap on the LBTC/BTC component — exactly the pattern AAVE has formalised for wstETH and LBTC.

The heartbeat check is enforced inside getUsdValue: if the feed is older than heartbeat seconds, the call reverts. New deals cannot open against a stale feed; liquidations can still proceed at the last sane price (AMINA decides via off-chain risk policy whether to accept).

L2 · risk

CollateralRegistry

The risk-parameter source of truth. One entry per (collateral token, supply token) pair.

struct Params {
    uint16 ltvBps;              // e.g. 8500 = 85%
    uint16 warningBps;          // e.g. 9000 = 90% LTV triggers warn
    uint16 partialLiqBps;       // e.g. 9300 = 93% LTV triggers partial liq
    uint16 fullLiqBps;          // e.g. 9500 = 95% LTV triggers full liq
    uint32 maxMaturity;         // seconds — cap on deal length
    uint16 maxRateBps;          // sanity cap on deal rate
    bool active;
}

mapping(bytes32 => Params) public paramsByPair;          // keccak(collateral, supply)
mapping(uint32 => bytes32) public versionKey;            // version → param key
uint32 public latestVersion;

Versioning, AAVE-v4 style. When AMINA tightens the LTV on Class B collateral, existing deals must not auto-liquidate. The current version is snapshotted into a deal at openDeal time; the deal carries that uint32 versionKey for its lifetime. The check is:

  • Risk-reducing actions (top-up collateral, partial repay) always use the deal's snapshot version.
  • Risk-increasing actions (none exist in this model post-creation, since deals are fixed at open) are not applicable.
  • New deals always use latestVersion.

This means AMINA can publish a tightened risk schedule without retroactively endangering live deals — and the on-chain history of versions is itself an audit log.

L3 · deal engine

DealRegistry

Append-only record of deal terms. A deal is created on-chain only after off-chain matching, signed by all three parties, and never mutated.

struct DealTerms {
    address lender;
    address borrower;
    address supplyToken;        // Token B (e.g. custody-issued USDC)
    address collateralToken;    // Token A (e.g. custody-issued BTC)
    uint128 principal;          // in supplyToken units
    uint128 collateralAmount;   // in collateralToken units
    uint32 rateBps;             // simple-interest annual rate
    uint64 startTs;
    uint64 maturityTs;
    uint32 collateralVersion;   // snapshot of CollateralRegistry version
    bytes32 termsHash;          // EIP-712 hash of all terms
}

mapping(bytes32 => DealTerms) public terms;              // dealId → terms

function recordDeal(
    DealTerms calldata t,
    bytes calldata lenderSig,
    bytes calldata borrowerSig,
    bytes calldata aminaSig                              // matching attestation
) external onlyRole(CURATOR) returns (bytes32 dealId);

Three signatures, not two. The lender and borrower each sign their bilateral consent. AMINA's signature is the brokerage attestation — the regulated record that this match was performed under its Securities Dealer licence. Without all three, the deal cannot be opened. This is the on-chain analogue of the trade confirmation in a TradFi repo.

Deal terms are immutable. Modifying a live deal (extension, restructure, novation) is modelled as closing the old deal and opening a new one, both happening atomically in a batch call. This preserves audit clarity.

L3 · deal engine

EscrowVault

Holds tokens. Has exactly one allowed caller: LendingEngine. All movement is through typed methods so that token IDs cannot accidentally cross between deals.

// Per-deal balance ledger
mapping(bytes32 => mapping(address => uint256)) public balanceOf;
//      dealId            token

function pullCollateral(bytes32 dealId, address from, uint256 amount) external onlyEngine;
function releaseCollateral(bytes32 dealId, address to, uint256 amount) external onlyEngine;
function pullSupply     (bytes32 dealId, address from, uint256 amount) external onlyEngine;
function releaseSupply  (bytes32 dealId, address to,   uint256 amount) external onlyEngine;

The single shared vault holds all deals' tokens but the per-deal ledger means no two deals can ever read or write each other's balance. This is the logical-isolation equivalent of Design A's physical isolation.

One subtle invariant: the sum of balanceOf[*][token] across all deals must always equal the vault's actual ERC-20 balance for token. The contract enforces this only on writes; an explicit syncCheck(token) view function lets governance reconcile periodically.

L3 · deal engine

LendingEngine

The state machine. Reads from every other contract; writes deal state.

enum DealState {
    None,
    Pending,        // recorded but tokens not yet posted
    Active,         // both legs posted, accruing interest
    Warned,         // LTV ≥ warning, 48h grace
    Liquidating,    // partial or full liquidation in progress
    Matured,        // maturity reached, awaiting repay
    Repaid,         // terminal: borrower repaid in full
    Liquidated,     // terminal: AMINA closed via custody
    Defaulted       // terminal: residual debt after liquidation
}

struct DealState_t {
    DealState state;
    uint128 outstanding;        // principal + accrued interest snapshot
    uint128 collateralPosted;   // current collateral balance
    uint64 lastTouchTs;         // last interest-snapshot time
    uint32 versionKey;          // snapshot from CollateralRegistry
}

mapping(bytes32 => DealState_t) public state;

Entry points (callable by parties to a specific deal):

FunctionCallerState transitions
activate(dealId)lender or borrowerPending → Active. Pulls collateral from borrower, pulls supply from lender, releases supply to borrower (atomic).
topUpCollateral(dealId, amount)borrowerActive/Warned → Active. Always allowed; never raises risk.
repay(dealId, amount)borrower (or anyone)Partial: stays in same state. Full: → Repaid. Pulls supply, releases collateral.
warn(dealId)LIQUIDATORActive → Warned. No token movement. Starts 48-h grace clock.
partialLiquidate(dealId, amount)LIQUIDATORWarned → Active (if cured) or Liquidating. Calls LiquidationHandler.
fullLiquidate(dealId)LIQUIDATORLiquidating → Liquidated or Defaulted.
settleMaturity(dealId)anyone after maturityActive/Warned → Matured. If unpaid after grace, LIQUIDATOR can call fullLiquidate.

Interest accrual model — simple, not compounding, snapshotted on every state change:

function _accrue(bytes32 dealId) internal {
    DealTerms storage t = registry.terms(dealId);
    DealState_t storage s = state[dealId];
    uint64 dt = uint64(block.timestamp) - s.lastTouchTs;
    uint256 add = uint256(t.principal) * t.rateBps * dt / (365 days * 10000);
    s.outstanding += uint128(add);
    s.lastTouchTs = uint64(block.timestamp);
}

Simple interest is sufficient because deals are short (typically 30–90 days) and the cost-of-capital convention in institutional repo is also simple-interest day-count. Skipping compounding shaves complexity and removes a class of rounding-edge bugs.

Health factor:

HF = collateralValueUSD × ltvBps / (outstandingValueUSD × 10000)

HF ≥ 1 means safe. warningBps, partialLiqBps, fullLiqBps from CollateralRegistry define the action thresholds, but only AMINA may pull the trigger — the contract permits liquidation when conditions are met, it does not allow anyone to perform it.

L4 · liquidation

LiquidationHandler

The privileged liquidation actor. Only addresses with the LIQUIDATOR role can call it. Three flows:

FlowTriggerWhat happens on-chain
WarnHF crosses warningBpsState → Warned. Event emitted, off-chain notification fires. No token movement. 48-hour grace clock starts.
PartialHF crosses partialLiqBps and grace expired (or borrower opt-in)AMINA computes amount to bring deal back to safe LTV. Engine releases that fraction of collateral to AMINA's settlement address. AMINA, off-chain, redeems at custody and uses the proceeds to reduce the deal's outstanding.
FullHF crosses fullLiqBps, or maturity passed with debt outstandingAll remaining collateral released to AMINA. Deal moves to Liquidated. If residual debt remains (collateral value < outstanding), deal moves to Defaulted and emits an event for off-chain accounting; protocol records the shortfall but does NOT socialise it.

Why no bonus / no auction. AMINA is the broker, not an arbitrageur. The economics of repaying the lender, redeeming the collateral, and selling it on a regulated venue happen entirely under AMINA's licence and balance sheet. The contract's job is only to deterministically authorise the collateral transfer when the threshold has been crossed.

Replay safety. Each liquidation step is identified by a monotonically increasing step counter on the deal. AMINA cannot accidentally trigger a partial liquidation twice; the engine rejects calls with a stale step number.

Operational risk to be aware of: the contract relies on AMINA to follow through on the off-chain redemption. If AMINA receives collateral on-chain but fails to credit the lender off-chain, the lender's remedy is a regulated complaint against AMINA, not an on-chain mechanism. This is intentional and matches the BNY Mellon analogy — the agent's reputation and licence is the enforcement layer. The contract's job ends at "collateral delivered to AMINA's settlement wallet."
L4 · settlement

SettlementRouter

A stateless event emitter that gives the off-chain custody integration a stable schema. Every meaningful settlement event (advance, repay, top-up, liquidation, redemption intent) is mirrored here with structured fields the custodian's listener can pick up.

event AdvanceIntent(bytes32 indexed dealId, address indexed token, uint256 amount, address to);
event RedemptionIntent(bytes32 indexed dealId, address indexed token, uint256 amount, address from);
event LiquidationIntent(bytes32 indexed dealId, address indexed collateralToken, uint256 amount, address aminaSettlement);
event MaturityReached(bytes32 indexed dealId, uint256 outstanding);
event Defaulted(bytes32 indexed dealId, uint256 shortfallUsd);

Splitting the event surface out of LendingEngine keeps that contract focused on state transitions and gives custodian engineering teams one well-defined ABI to integrate against. The events also become the canonical reconciliation feed for the off-chain dashboard.

8. Token integration — supply & collateral tokens

Both Token A (collateral) and Token B (supply) are issued by regulated custodians. They are not plain ERC-20s — they carry their own KYC/KYB gating, typically via the ERC-3643 standard (T-REX, Tokeny's framework) or a custodian's proprietary allowlist.

How the contracts handle this

  1. No assumption of permissionless transferability. All token movement uses SafeERC20 wrappers that bubble up the underlying compliance revert. If the custodian's allowlist rejects a transfer (e.g., the protocol vault is not on the allowlist for that token), the engine reverts and emits a structured event so off-chain operations can fix the allowlist out-of-band.
  2. The vault must itself be allowlisted on every supply and collateral token. The launch checklist requires that for every IssuerRegistry entry, the custodian has added EscrowVault to the token's allowlist. IssuerRegistry.activate(token) can include an on-chain check (call token.canTransfer(address(vault))) where the ERC-3643 interface is available.
  3. Pause propagation. If a custodian pauses a token at the issuer level, the protocol's IssuerRegistry.paused flag should be flipped to match. Existing deals already hold balances and can still be liquidated; new deals using the paused token are blocked.
Why not roll a custom ERC-20 wrapper here. Tempting, but the regulatory model fundamentally rests on the custodian's mint authority. Wrapping a Fireblocks- or AMINA-issued token in a protocol-owned wrapper would force the protocol to take on the redemption obligation — which is exactly what we are designed to avoid.

9. Privacy & on-chain footprint

The brief's §5 emphasises that the chain layer should not leak counterparty identity. Three properties make this manageable:

  1. One escrow vault, many deals. Lender X and borrower Y do not share an on-chain address other than the dealId entry, and that entry's only public link is to wallets which custody (not the chain) maps to legal entities.
  2. Fresh-address discipline at the wallet layer. Custodians can mint Token A and Token B to a per-deal sub-account address. The wallet address itself becomes pseudonymous. The protocol does not need to know this is happening — it just sees ERC-20 transfers.
  3. Deal terms that need not be public can be moved off-chain. termsHash binds the contract to an off-chain document; the on-chain row only needs the fields the engine actually computes against (principal, rate, maturity, collateral, parties). Pricing footnotes, side letters, etc. can live in the hashed packet.

Stronger privacy (commitment-scheme based, ZK-friendly) is feasible but out of scope for v1. The architecture leaves it open: the engine reads from DealRegistry through an interface, and that interface could later be backed by a commitment-scheme deal store rather than the plain-storage version.

10. Upgradeability strategy

AAVE v4 makes Hubs immutable and Spokes upgradeable. We adopt a similar split, calibrated to the bilateral model:

ContractUpgradeable?Why
RoleManager (OZ AM)NoStandard, audited. Roles can be re-bound without redeploying.
KYBGatewayUUPSKYB policy evolves; schema may grow (e.g., tier flags).
IssuerRegistryUUPSSchema may grow (e.g., per-issuer settlement-time fields).
OracleRouterUUPSComposite-adapter shapes change as new tokens arrive.
CollateralRegistryUUPSVersioning + new parameter fields.
DealRegistryImmutableAppend-only record of legal obligations. Migrating to a v2 deal record requires a new contract address and is itself a documented event.
EscrowVaultImmutableCustody of value — keep dead simple, never upgrade. Migration is via a one-way deal-by-deal evacuation.
LendingEngineUUPS (with timelock)State machine may need to evolve; timelock + multisig protects users.
LiquidationHandlerUUPS (with timelock)Liquidation policy may evolve.
SettlementRouterUUPSEvent schema may grow.

Immutability of DealRegistry and EscrowVault is the single strongest guarantee the protocol gives institutional counterparties: the record of your loan and the location of your collateral cannot be silently rewritten by governance. Upgrades to the engine cannot rewrite history or move tokens.

14. Extension paths (post-v1)

  1. Debt-obligation NFTs. Add ERC-721 collateral support in CollateralRegistry + a small token-kind extension. The engine treats them as zero-LTV collateral for HF purposes, with default flow handled off-chain via LCIA arbitration as the brief envisions.
  2. RWA-backed deals. Already supported: tokenised treasuries (BUIDL, BENJI, USDY) are ERC-20 tokens issued by regulated custodians. Just add them to IssuerRegistry with the appropriate parameters.
  3. DeFi liquidity channel. Wrap a basket of similar deals into a Mellow- or Morpho-style ERC-4626 vault that institutional retail can buy. The vault buys the supply side of many deals. Implementing this is purely above the engine — no engine change needed.
  4. Per-counterparty bespoke escrow (Design A migration). Add a DealEscrowFactory that, when a CURATOR-flagged "bespoke" deal is recorded, deploys a minimal proxy. The engine's logic remains; the storage of certain deals just lives in a different physical address.
  5. Stronger privacy. Replace DealRegistry's plain-storage backend with a commitment-scheme variant. ZK-friendly construction. The engine's interface stays the same.
  6. Cross-Spoke composability with AAVE. A custom AAVE v4 Vault Spoke could treat a P2PxAmina deal's lender position as collateral (deal note → AAVE), giving institutional lenders an exit ramp without breaking the bilateral model. Speculative.

15. Summary

The protocol described in the v0.7 brief is structurally close to a TradFi tri-party repo, not to AAVE or Compound. The right contract architecture acknowledges that:

  • Use AAVE v4's structural lessons — separation of governance, registry, risk, and core engine; per-asset oracle isolation; emergency state semantics; dynamic risk versioning — without inheriting its pooled-economy machinery.
  • Use Compound v3's storage discipline and the Configurator-as-factory pattern, without per-market deployments.
  • Keep the core engine small (≈260 LOC) and the escrow trivial (≈140 LOC). Push complexity to the registries and to the off-chain layer where it belongs.
  • Hold the immutability line on DealRegistry and EscrowVault; everything else is UUPS-upgradeable with timelock.
  • The total Solidity footprint comes in at roughly 1,340 lines — inside the v0.7 budget, leaving headroom for libraries and the inevitable late-stage edge cases.
One question the brief leaves open and the architecture resolves: who, on-chain, attests that a deal was matched under AMINA's brokerage licence? Answer: AMINA itself, via the third EIP-712 signature on DealRegistry.recordDeal. This makes the regulatory chain of custody verifiable on-chain without exposing the matching engine's internals.
